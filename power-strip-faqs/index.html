Power strip questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

The most important things to know: first, a surge protector is what most people actually need, it adds voltage-spike defense to a basic power strip. Second, pay attention to joule ratings (higher is better for valuable gear) and clamp voltage. Third, never daisy-chain strips; plug high-draw appliances like space heaters directly into a wall outlet.

What’s the difference between a power strip and a surge protector?

A basic power strip is just a multi-outlet extension cord with an on/off switch. It does nothing to protect electronics from voltage spikes. A surge protector contains internal components, usually a metal oxide varistor (MOV). that absorb excess voltage during a surge, like from lightning or utility grid switching. If you’re plugging in a cheap lamp or phone charger, a simple strip is fine. But for computers, TVs, or anything with a circuit board, buy a surge protector. Look for one that clearly states its clamping voltage (how fast it responds) and joule rating (how much energy it can absorb before failing). A typical home-office surge protector from a reputable brand will handle everyday surges well.

How many outlets do I really need?

Count the devices you actually plug in near that strip, not the ones you might add someday, but the ones that are there now. A standard 6-outlet strip handles a desk setup: monitor, computer, phone charger, lamp, printer. If you have a large entertainment center or a workshop with multiple tools, go for 8 or 12 outlets. Also consider spacing. Many wall warts (power adapters) are bulky and block adjacent outlets. Look for strips with “widely spaced” or “rotating” outlets, or models that have a few side-facing plugs. Some strips include USB ports for charging phones and tablets, which can free up AC outlets, but note that USB power is limited, great for overnight charging but slow for high-wattage devices.

What does joule rating mean, and how high should it go?

The joule rating tells you how much surge energy the protector can absorb before it’s worn out. A rating of 1,000 joules can handle multiple small surges or one big one. For basic electronics, a lamp, a clock, a few hundred joules is fine. For a desktop computer, gaming console, or home theater receiver, look for at least 1,000 to 2,000 joules. Keep in mind that every surge degrades the MOVs; after absorbing several surges, the protector may stop working without any obvious sign. Higher joule ratings typically mean a longer service life. Many quality protectors also have an “indicator light” that lets you know its protection is still active, if that light goes out, replace the unit.

Can I plug a power strip into a power strip?

No, this is called daisy-chaining, and it’s a fire hazard. The first strip’s circuit breaker or fuse is designed to handle only its own load. Plugging a second strip into it can overload the first strip, causing overheating or tripping the breaker. The National Electrical Code forbids daisy-chaining in most commercial and residential settings. Instead, use one properly-rated strip that has enough outlets, or run a separate extension cord from a different wall outlet. If you truly need more outlets than the room’s electrical setup provides, consider having an electrician add a new outlet or install a permanent power bar that meets code.

Are USB ports on power strips safe and convenient?

Yes, when bought from a reputable brand like Belkin, Anker, or Tripp Lite. USB ports on a power strip are essentially a small multi-port charger integrated into the unit. They typically provide a total of 2.4 to 4.8 amps across all ports, enough to charge phones and tablets slowly overnight, but rarely enough to fast-charge newer laptops or tablets with high-wattage needs. One trade-off: the USB ports are usually not surge-protected in the same way the AC outlets are. Check the specs; some models offer USB surge protection, others do not. Also, if the USB circuit fails, you might lose those ports but the AC outlets still work. For critical devices, use the AC outlets with a separate USB charger that includes its own surge suppression.

Frequently asked questions

How do I know if a power strip is overloaded?

Most power strips have a built-in circuit breaker that trips if the load exceeds its rating, typically 15 amps for household strips. If the strip clicks off, you’ve overloaded it. Also watch for signs: a warm cord or unit, buzzing sound, or flickering lights on the strip itself.

Should I plug a surge protector into a GFCI outlet?

Yes, that’s fine. A GFCI outlet protects against ground faults in wet areas; a surge protector protects against voltage spikes. They do different jobs, and using both in series is safe. However, if the GFCI trips due to a fault, the surge protector downstream will lose power too.

Can a power strip be used outdoors?

Only if it’s specifically rated for outdoor use. Indoor power strips lack weatherproofing and can short out or corrode. Outdoor-rated strips have sealed outlets, a more rugged housing, and often a longer cord. Even then, protect the strip from direct rain and never submerge it.

Do power strips need to be replaced regularly?

Surge protectors degrade with each surge they absorb. A good rule is to replace them every two to three years, or sooner if the indicator light goes out (on models that have one). Basic power strips without surge protection can last indefinitely, but check for damaged cords or outlets that feel loose.

What appliances should never go into a power strip?

High-wattage appliances like space heaters, air conditioners, refrigerators, microwaves, and hair dryers should never be plugged into a power strip. These devices draw heavy, continuous current that can easily exceed the strip’s rating and cause overheating or fire. Plug them directly into a wall outlet.

Is there a difference between a power strip and an extension cord?

Yes. An extension cord is just a length of wire with a plug on one end and one or more outlets on the other; it usually has no switch or circuit breaker. A power strip has a built-in switch and often includes surge protection or a breaker. Never use an extension cord where you need permanent extra outlets; use a power strip designed for that purpose.

In shortChoosing the right power strip comes down to a few simple checks: make sure it’s a surge protector if you’re connecting sensitive electronics, confirm it has enough joule ratings for your gear, avoid daisy-chaining, and never plug high-wattage appliances into one. Read the package label, manufacturers have to list ampacity, surge rating, and any certifications (like UL listing). A few minutes of comparison now can save you from fried devices or an electrical fire later.
